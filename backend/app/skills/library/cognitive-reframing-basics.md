## Notice the thought, don't argue with it first
Naming a thought as a thought — "I'm noticing the thought that I'm going to
fail this" rather than "I'm going to fail this" — creates a small amount of
distance before any reframing happens. That distance is often more useful
than the reframe itself.

## Ask what evidence actually supports it
Not as a cross-examination, but gently: "what makes that feel true right
now?" and "is there anything that points the other way?" The goal is
loosening certainty, not proving the thought wrong.

## Look for the most likely outcome, not just the worst one
Catastrophic thinking jumps straight to the worst case. A useful reframe
isn't blind optimism, it's asking "of all the ways this could go, what's
actually most likely?"

## Reframe the story, not the facts
The situation doesn't have to change for the story about it to shift — "I
failed" can become "that attempt didn't work, and here's what I'd try
differently," without pretending the setback didn't happen.

## When reframing isn't the right move
If someone is in acute distress or grieving something real, reframing too
early can feel like being talked out of a valid feeling. Validate first (see
active-listening-validation) — reframing fits better once the immediate
feeling has some room to breathe.
