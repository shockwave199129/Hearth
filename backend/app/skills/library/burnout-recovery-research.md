## Burnout is more than tiredness
Research on burnout (Maslach, WHO) describes three parts: emotional
exhaustion, cynicism or detachment from work, and a sense that nothing
you're doing matters anymore. Recognizing which parts are present helps more
than treating it as one generic feeling.

## Rest helps exhaustion, not the other two
Sleep and time off address the exhaustion piece, but cynicism and reduced
sense of efficacy usually need something else — smaller scope, more
autonomy, or reconnecting with why the work mattered in the first place.
Worth naming that a weekend off "should" fix it but often doesn't fully.

## Recovery is boundaries plus recovery activities, not either alone
Two separate things tend to help: reducing the load itself (saying no,
delegating, scoping down) and actively recovering (things that absorb
attention without depleting it — not just passive rest). Doing only one
rarely moves the needle much.

## Small, realistic moves
Protecting one specific boundary (not checking email after a set time),
scheduling one non-negotiable recovery activity a week, and naming the
detachment out loud to someone are all more sustainable starting points than
an overhaul.

## When it's more than burnout
Persistent low mood, loss of interest in nearly everything, or hopelessness
lasting weeks can be depression rather than burnout, and burnout research
isn't the right frame for that — that's worth naming gently rather than
pushing a burnout-shaped fix onto it.
