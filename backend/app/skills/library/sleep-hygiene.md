## Consistent wake time over consistent bedtime
A fixed wake time (even after a bad night) does more for sleep regularity
than forcing an exact bedtime — the body's clock anchors more strongly to
when you get up than when you lie down.

## The bed is for sleep, not for lying awake worrying
If you're awake and frustrated after ~20 minutes, getting up and doing
something quiet and low-stimulation elsewhere, then coming back once sleepy,
helps break the association between bed and wakefulness.

## Wind-down matters more than any single trick
Screens, caffeine late in the day, and going straight from stimulating
activity to trying to sleep all work against the body's wind-down. A short,
repeated pre-sleep routine (even 10 minutes) signals the transition more
reliably than any one "hack."

## Racing thoughts at night
Writing down the thought or the next day's to-do list before bed (a "brain
dump") can reduce the urge to keep mentally rehearsing it — see also
journaling-prompts for a lightweight version of this.

## When it's a longer-standing problem
Occasional bad nights are normal. Sleep trouble most nights for weeks, or
sleep problems tangled up with a lot of anxiety or low mood, is worth naming
as something to bring to a doctor or therapist rather than something a habit
tweak alone will fix.
