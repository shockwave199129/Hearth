## Resentment is often a signal a boundary is missing
Recurring resentment toward the same person or situation is frequently a
sign that something needs to be said or changed, not just tolerated longer.
Worth naming that pattern rather than only addressing the latest instance.

## State it plainly, without over-justifying
A boundary doesn't need a long defense — "I can't take this on right now"
is a complete sentence. Over-explaining often invites negotiation on
something that isn't actually up for negotiation.

## Separate the boundary from the relationship
"I need evenings without work messages" is about the boundary, not a verdict
on the other person. Framing it that way tends to land as less
confrontational without softening it into nothing.

## Expect discomfort, especially at first
Boundaries that are new (especially with people used to a different pattern)
often get pushback initially. That pushback doesn't mean the boundary was
wrong — consistency over the first few instances is what makes it hold.

## A simple structure to offer
Describe the situation briefly, say what you need, and state it as a
decision rather than a request when appropriate: "When X happens, I need Y.
Going forward, I'm going to do Z." Adapt tone to the relationship — this is
a scaffold, not a script to recite.
