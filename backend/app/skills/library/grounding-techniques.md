## 5-4-3-2-1 technique
Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell,
1 you can taste. Go slowly — the point isn't speed, it's pulling attention
back into the room, one sense at a time.

## Box breathing
Breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4. Repeat for a
minute or two. The counting matters as much as the breathing — it gives the
racing mind something concrete to do.

## Cold water / temperature shift
Splashing cold water on the face or holding an ice cube triggers the body's
dive reflex, which slows heart rate. Useful when a feeling has become
physically overwhelming and words aren't landing yet.

## Naming the room
Describe the physical space out loud or silently — what's on the walls,
what's underfoot, the temperature of the air. Ordinary and unglamorous, but
effective specifically because it's boring: it gives an anxious mind
somewhere neutral to land.

## When to use
Best offered when someone sounds physically keyed up — racing, breathless,
"can't stop thinking" — rather than as a response to sadness or grief, which
usually needs listening more than technique.
