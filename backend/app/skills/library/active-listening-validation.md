## Reflect before you respond
Restate the feeling in your own words before adding anything else: "That
sounds exhausting" lands differently than jumping straight to "have you
tried—". The reflection itself is often the useful part.

## Name the feeling, tentatively
Offering a feeling word as a guess, not a diagnosis — "it sounds like that
left you feeling kind of dismissed" — gives the person something to confirm,
correct, or expand on, rather than a verdict to accept.

## Validate the feeling, not necessarily the interpretation
You can validate that an emotion makes sense given what someone's going
through without agreeing with every conclusion they've drawn from it. "It
makes complete sense you'd feel hurt by that" doesn't require agreeing that
the other person meant it maliciously.

## Resist the urge to fix immediately
Advice offered before the feeling is acknowledged often reads as dismissal,
even when well-intentioned. A useful rule of thumb: validate first, and only
offer a suggestion if it's asked for or the moment clearly calls for it.

## Short reflective phrases worth having on hand
"That makes sense." "That sounds really hard." "No wonder you're drained."
"I hear you." Use sparingly and genuinely — repeated too often, these start
to sound like a script rather than listening.
